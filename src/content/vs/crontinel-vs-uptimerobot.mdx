UptimeRobot and Crontinel solve different problems. UptimeRobot tells you whether your site is reachable from the outside. Crontinel tells you whether your Laravel background jobs, queues, and scheduled tasks are actually running. Most Laravel applications need both.

## What UptimeRobot Does Well

UptimeRobot is one of the most popular uptime monitoring services for good reason. It checks your URLs from external servers around the world and alerts you when something goes down. The core feature set is solid:

- HTTP/HTTPS monitoring with response time tracking
- Ping, port, and keyword checks
- A generous free tier with 50 monitors at 5-minute intervals
- Status pages you can share with your team or customers
- Integrations with Slack, email, webhooks, PagerDuty, and more

For pure HTTP uptime monitoring, UptimeRobot is hard to beat. If your site returns a 200, you know the web layer is healthy.

## What UptimeRobot Cannot See

The problem is that a 200 response tells you nothing about what happens behind that HTTP layer. Your site can return a perfect 200 while:

- Your email queue has 14,000 stuck jobs
- A Horizon supervisor crashed two hours ago
- The nightly report scheduler silently stopped running
- A critical cron job is failing with exit code 1 every time

UptimeRobot monitors the front door. It has no visibility into the work happening in the background. For a Laravel app that depends on queues and scheduled tasks, that gap can be costly.

## The Fundamental Difference

UptimeRobot uses **external HTTP probing**. It hits your endpoint from outside and checks the response. This is the right approach for uptime monitoring, and UptimeRobot executes it well.

Crontinel uses **internal Laravel monitoring**. It runs inside your application, watches the scheduler event loop, reads queue depths from Redis or your database, and tracks Horizon supervisor state directly. It knows whether `app:send-invoices` ran at 3:00 AM and whether it succeeded or failed.

These are complementary approaches, not competing ones.

## When to Use UptimeRobot

Use UptimeRobot when you need to know whether your site is reachable from the internet. It handles HTTP monitoring, SSL certificate expiry alerts, and response time tracking. If your primary concern is "can users load the page," UptimeRobot covers that.

## When to Use Crontinel

Use Crontinel when you need to know whether your Laravel background work is healthy. If your app processes jobs through queues, runs scheduled tasks via the scheduler, or uses Horizon, Crontinel watches all of that. The open-source package gives you a local dashboard and CLI checks at no cost. The SaaS layer adds hosted alerting and history if you want it.

## When to Use Both

Most production Laravel applications should run both. UptimeRobot covers the external layer. Crontinel covers the internal layer. Together, they give you full visibility: you know the site is up and you know the background work is running.

A common setup looks like this: UptimeRobot pings your health endpoint every 5 minutes, while Crontinel monitors your scheduler and queues from inside the app every minute.

## Installation Comparison

**UptimeRobot** requires no code changes. You add a URL in their dashboard and monitoring starts immediately.

**Crontinel** installs as a Laravel package:

```bash
composer require crontinel/laravel
php artisan crontinel:install
```

After installing, you get the local dashboard at `/crontinel` and can run health checks via:

```bash
php artisan crontinel:check
```

Connecting to the SaaS for hosted alerting takes one additional env variable: your API key from app.crontinel.com.

## Two Tools, One Stack

UptimeRobot is excellent at what it does. Crontinel handles what UptimeRobot cannot see. Running both means your HTTP layer and your background job layer are monitored independently, so nothing fails silently. For any Laravel app that relies on queues or scheduled tasks, that combination is the baseline for production readiness.
